Reflecting on Foundations

2024/04/19 | Week Five of Dev Academy | by Sam Whitmore

Things I Have Learned

For much of my life, I have been fixated on being likeable and, in doing so, have not become a very assertive presence in this world. Throughout Foundations, however, I have felt myself becoming more vocal and proactive. I feel less rigid; less likely to second-guess my opinions, and therefore more willing to go with the flow and speak my mind.

I have been living alone throughout Foundations, too, which I believe must've had an impact on my changing behaviour. Quite simply, I have loved the peace and quiet that this lifestyle affords me; I consider it the greatest luxury I've ever enjoyed. I used to live in Queenstown and, before that, in Wellington – with flatmates that I still consider friends – and I can say with absolute certainty that I would not be finding the same level of success in this program if I were still living in either of those circumstances.

Living alone, there's nobody to hold me accountable but myself. In this, I've learned that I will never feel completely ready to begin working; to immerse myself in the unknown. There will always be a part of me that wants to cut myself some more slack, instead of pulling the weight. Through this truth, I'm learning to arrive to work with all of myself including the parts of myself that are apprehensive. That way, when the "better" part of me succeeds, the "worse" part of me can enjoy that success, too. In other words, my success won't be in spite of my "worse" tendencies but, rather, coexists with those tendencies.

Values, Reason, Self-Awareness and Empathy

Learning anything (but particularly programming) is both challenging and confronting. By near-definition, they lead the learner to feel inadequate (at least to begin with) – which is an inherently stressful situation to put one's self into.

In this environment, values bring meaning and direction to the otherwise-senseless stress. For example, if one values basic resources and security, as we all do, then the stressful suffering incurred within a learning environment can be understood as a necessary burden for progression toward a life with plenty of resources and security.

In fact, Viktor Frankl would argue that the stress ceases to be sufferable. In Man's Search for Meaning, Frankl quotes Friedrich Nietzsche, who said "he who has a why to live can bear almost any how."

The quote above speaks to the important of having a reason, which wraps the discourse back around to rationality. Programming is a hyper-rational realm of thought and productivity, and thus any immersion into the realm ought to be reasonable and purposeful.

The question thus becomes: why am I putting myself through this?

Pragmatically, I'm here for the reasons listed above: I need resources to survive and would like to secure those means for existence; but, furthermore, I want to make a contribution to this world; to restore it, somehow – and to do this I must grow my skillset. It is this meaningful, valuable, and reasonable quest of mine that will bring me resilience and drive as I am faced with challenge, going forward.

Similarly, valuing the likes of honesty, humility and integrity will ground the learning journey upon a foundation of truth, which is also the foundation of reason. By holding these virtues, I will enjoy a smoother learning curve as I engage myself with appropriate challenges at appropriate times. This point echoes Aesop's fable of the Tortoise and the Hare: slow and steady wins the race; I will not bite off more than I can chew.

To have empathy is to recognise that all of life is on a similar pathway to yours, but never the same path. In some respects, you may be ahead of them, and in others, they may be ahead of you; but that is ultimately subjective...

Empathy breeds compassion and humility, each of which bolster one's ability to connect and coexist with other subjective minds. It also endorses one's agency, encouraging a sense of mutual responsibility to one-another. I like to believe that I am not more-or-less worthy for being the person that I am, and that all life is inherently innocent. By extension, I believe that nobody is beyond rehabilitation and that anybody can grow through their current state of mind. We are products of our environment, and this too shall pass.

Within the context of programming, I ought not to feel clever or defeated depending on the current situation I'm in. Instead, I should feel both emotions simultaneously; both the genius and the fool. This coinhabitance of emotionality should be extended to the people I meet along my learning journey, regardless of how they present themselves to me. Everybody can make mistakes, and I should warmly laugh at my own whenever I make one.

Surpising Aspects of the Core Learnings

I don't think I've conceptually learned anything new throughout the core learning aspects of Foundations, but I have enjoyed putting them into practice.

This is particularly true for the introductory phase of Foundations. I am especially proud of the vulnerability I shared and the resonating impact it seemed to have on the other contributions that followed my own. In that, I suppose I am surprised at how quickly I have felt a genuine connection and camaraderie with my peers within my cohort, and this connection is largely due to the prompts throughout the core learning aspects of Foundations.

The greatest opportunities to develop connections with my peers have been the Listening and Looping sessions, from the second half of Foundations. These have been very challenging for me, as I have an ADHD diagnosis and thus anxiety is an ever-present part of my life. Additionally, I'd consider myself a socially ambitious person; perhaps, self-aware to a destructive degree, so I take both responsibilities of listening and looping very seriously – and with that comes anxiety.

Suffice to say, when my conversational parter pours themselves into their words, I want to reflect their sentiments back to them as accurately as I can. I feel that the world is an incredibly lonely; sadly superficial place, largely due to our limited capcity to hold space for others and our ineloquence when trying to express our impressions.

The underlying belief is that brings me anxiety is: to the extent that I am unable to properly distill and then reflect my partner's sentiments, I will re-demonstrate their isolation inherent to the human experience.

And yet, despite those beliefs, I thoroughly enjoyed the Listening and Looping practice sessions prompted by the core learning component of Foundations. As daunting as it is to truly listen to someone, it is an absolute honour to do so and I am proud of the attempt I gave in each session. It is a skill that I would like tp practice much more, and so would encourage Dev Academy to place a greater emphasis on this, going forward!

Why the Focus on Core Learning?

It makes sense as to why Dev Academy places so much focus on their student's internal core, alongside their technical skills.

Programming is such a pure form of communication so, when a difficulty is encountered, it is very easy for individuals to become hard on their selves for finding such difficulty. It is important to have self-awareness and empathy toward other selves in this industry because we are not hyper-rational beings; we are human and inherently fallible. In a nutshell, programming has such a steep learning curve that this journey can often feel like running up against a brick wall. To progress, programmers ought to smooth out the learning curve, which is only achieved through the likes of humility, integrity and self-awareness.

In addition, Dev Academy offers NZQA credits for completing this course, and NZQA surely has requirements that ground the technical lessons within our culture and society. By offering the core learning component alongside the technical component, Dev Academy brings a more robust product to market. The NZQA credits is what allows this course to be eligible for StudyLink's student loan program, which is the only reason I have been able to afford to participate in this program, and I am sure that I'm not alone in this. So the core component of the program widens the scope of the market that they are catering to. Essentially, it's good business to offer a core component.

Does the Time Spent on Core Feel Wasted?

For me, personally, core has felt like a great waste of time, as I believe I already have considerable self-awareness. I have not learned anything new from the core learning program, thus far, and to be honest, I don't expect to, going forward. From some discussion on the Discord server, it seems that I am not alone in this, either; the cohort seems to be generally quite self-aware and knowledgable about the terminology introduced throughout the Foundational core component. In this, the core lessons felt somewhat trivial and, perhaps, tokenistic – as if Dev Academy was simply checking a box to satisfy NZQA's requirements for funding and accreditation, etc.

I guess the sentiment I'm hoping to convey here is "if you're going to do something, do it well" – and I'm not sure that the core lessons would be all that relevant to anybody who has done a bit of reading on the topic before – which should be most of the course participants, as they're a cohort of people that have signed up for a coding course, so you might expect that these are people that already believe they have the ability to grow their expertise...

In saying that, I'm very glad that we have had the opportunity to put the core lessons into practice, as I don't believe there is such a thing as too much practice in this realm of thought. I'm really glad that the practical focus on core learnings has been established so emphatically. I feel that everybody is willing to have meaningful conversations about their core, and that will undoubtedly pay dividends, going forward. Just as the technical foundational learnings were designed to get everybody on the same page, the practical, foundational core learnings have surely done the same for our spirits, heading into Bootcamp.
